GODMAX Diagnostics: Covariance Validation Report

Notebook origin: diagnostics_tests.ipynb Branch: dev-diagnostics Main probes: gy, gg, ky

This report documents a systematic validation of the GODMAX covariance pipeline. The starting issue is that multi-probe covariance matrices develop a small number of negative eigenvalues, whereas single-probe cases remain positive semidefinite (PSD). The aim is to determine whether those negative modes indicate a physical inconsistency, an implementation error, or a harmless numerical effect.

The tests proceed from the 3-D halo-model power spectra to Limber projection, angular consistency, covariance assembly, and eigenvalue diagnostics. An independent reconstruction of the Gaussian covariance using the Knox formula is used as a final cross-check.

Main conclusion The covariance implementation is correct. The Knox-based covariance reproduces the GODMAX Gaussian covariance to floating-point precision, and the observed negative eigenvalues are tiny numerical artifacts (typically at the $10^{-27}$ level), not signs of a physical or coding error.

Validation strategy

The validation is organized as a sequence of independent tests, each targeting a different stage of the pipeline. This structure makes it possible to separate physical consistency checks from purely numerical ones.

Setup

All tests were run using the standard GODMAX class chain base_class → Profiles → get_Pkz → get_Cl → get_cov. The full pipeline executed successfully, and the resulting objects were used consistently throughout the report.

3-D power spectra and Cauchy–Schwarz consistency

Before looking at angular quantities, we test whether the halo-model 3-D spectra are internally consistent. The relevant condition is

$$ r_{xy}(k,z) \equiv \frac{P_{xy}^2(k,z)}{P_{xx}(k,z)P_{yy}(k,z)} \le 1. $$

If this fails, then some inconsistency is already present before Limber projection.

3-D Cauchy–Schwarz ratios in the Limber-relevant band --------------------------------------------------- (ky, kk, yy) : max r = 0.873 OK (gy, gg, yy) : max r = 1.031 VIOLATED (gk, gg, kk) : max r = 0.961 OK
Finding The only clear violation is the $(gy,gg,yy)$ triplet. This already indicates that the source of the issue is not general to all fields, but tied specifically to the interplay between galaxy and tSZ spectra.
Fig. 2a. Shapes and amplitudes of the six 3-D power spectra used in the analysis.
Fig. 2b. 3-D Cauchy–Schwarz ratios. Only the $(gy,gg,yy)$ combination exceeds unity.

Limber integral validation

The next step is to verify that the Limber projection itself is correct. The angular spectra are recomputed independently in NumPy using the same input spectra and kernels, and are then compared against the GODMAX outputs.

$$ C_\ell^{AB} = \int \frac{W_A(\chi)W_B(\chi)}{\chi^2} P_{AB}\!\left(\frac{\ell+\tfrac12}{\chi},z(\chi)\right)\frac{d\chi}{dz}\,dz. $$
Manual Limber reconstruction vs GODMAX ------------------------------------- kk : max rel. error = 1.3e-16 ky : max rel. error = 2.1e-16 yy : max rel. error = 8.7e-17 gg : max rel. error = 1.9e-16
Result The Limber projection is numerically exact at machine precision for all tested probes. This rules out the projection machinery as the origin of the covariance issue.
Fig. 3. Manual Limber integrals overlaid on GODMAX angular spectra. Residuals stay at machine precision.

Angular Cauchy–Schwarz consistency

After projection, the same consistency can be tested directly at the level of the angular spectra:

$$ r^\ell_{xy} \equiv \frac{\left[C_\ell^{xy}\right]^2}{C_{\ell,\mathrm{tot}+N}^{xx}C_{\ell,\mathrm{tot}+N}^{yy}} \le 1. $$
Angular Cauchy–Schwarz summary ------------------------------ ky : all bins satisfy the bound gy : bin 1 reaches r_max = 1.0312 at ell ≈ 506.5 gk : all bins satisfy the bound
Finding The projected violation is small and confined to the first $gy$ lens bin. This mirrors the 3-D result and confirms that the inconsistency survives projection, but only weakly.
Fig. 4. Angular Cauchy–Schwarz ratios for $\kappa y$, $gy$, and $g\kappa$. Only $gy$ bin 1 exceeds unity.

Baryonic suppression factor analysis

The suppression factor is defined as

$$ S(k,z) \equiv \frac{P_{\mathrm{halofit}}^{\mathrm{DM}}(k,z)}{P_{\mathrm{NFW,tot}}(k,z)}. $$

It is best interpreted here as a halo-model accuracy correction for matter-like spectra, rather than a generic baryonic correction applicable to every field.

SpectrumSuppressed by $S(k,z)$?Interpretation
$P_{mm}$YesTarget of the matter calibration
$P_{gm}, P_{gg}$YesApproximate extension to biased tracers
$P_{ym}, P_{gy}, P_{yy}$NoPressure-sector spectra are already baryonified in the BCMP/HSE chain
Interpretation The asymmetry is now clear: $P_{gg}$ is reduced by $S(k,z)$, while $P_{gy}$ and $P_{yy}$ are left untouched. That is exactly the pattern required to produce the $(gy,gg,yy)$ Cauchy–Schwarz violation.
Suppression factor in the Limber-relevant range --------------------------------------------- Typical median values of S(k,z): 0.89 – 0.99 depending on redshift Deepest suppression occurs at larger k, reaching roughly 30% in the most affected region

Fix A: remove $P_\mathrm{sup}$ from $P_{gg}$

Fix A removes the suppression from $P_{gg}$. This is physically attractive because $S(k,z)$ is calibrated against the matter field, not against biased HOD tracers.

Interpretation This change increases $C_\ell^{gg}$ as expected and restores the natural ordering between $gy$, $gg$, and $yy$ without introducing an unphysical modification to the tSZ sector.

Eigenvalue diagnostics

The key numerical question is whether the negative eigenvalues are large enough to matter. They are not.

Negative eigenvalue summary -------------------------- default : min eig = -6.813e-27, n_neg = 7 fixA : min eig = -6.453e-27, n_neg = 6 fixB : min eig = -6.768e-27, n_neg = 8
Key observation The negative modes are tiny compared with the positive spectrum. They sit many orders of magnitude below the dominant covariance scale and are fully consistent with floating-point cancellation.

The worst mode is dominated by the gy bin_1_0 direction around $\ell \approx 718$, which is exactly where the Gaussian and non-Gaussian terms nearly cancel.

Impact of PSD clipping

Since the remaining negative eigenvalues are purely numerical, we also test whether projecting the covariance onto the PSD cone by clipping negative eigenvalues changes the matrix in any physically meaningful way. It does not.

The only physically meaningful inconsistency identified in this study is the asymmetric application of the suppression factor: $P_{gg}$ is suppressed while $P_{gy}$ and $P_{yy}$ are not. This creates a small Cauchy–Schwarz violation in the $(gy,gg,yy)$ sector. However, this effect does not explain the negative covariance eigenvalues.

The negative eigenvalues arise from floating-point near-cancellation in a very small subspace of the full covariance and remain far below the physical scale of the matrix. The independent Knox reconstruction confirms that the Gaussian covariance is assembled correctly, and PSD clipping confirms that removing the negative modes has no practical effect on the covariance structure.

Recommended resolution Treat the negative eigenvalues as harmless numerical artifacts and regularize them if required for downstream numerical stability. For the physical modeling, Fix A is the better-motivated option: do not apply $P_\mathrm{sup}$ to $P_{gg}$ unless a tracer-specific calibration justifies it.
