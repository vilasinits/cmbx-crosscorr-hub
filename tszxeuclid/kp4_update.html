DR1-KP-CMBX-4 — Pipeline Update

Cross-correlation between the thermal Sunyaev-Zeldovich signal and the Euclid probes
Léa Dumilly 10 February 2026 Université Paris-Saclay / IAS Euclid CMBx SWG

Supervisors: Laura Salvati · Nabila Aghanim · Giulio Fabbian

Introduction & Scientific Motivation

The Universe began with the Big Bang ∼13.8 Gyr ago. After recombination (~380,000 yr), the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) was emitted as a nearly-perfect blackbody at $T \approx 3000\,\mathrm{K}$, which we observe today at $T_0 = 2.725\,\mathrm{K}$. Since then, gravity has caused baryonic matter to collapse into the Large-Scale Structure (LSS) of filaments, voids, and galaxy clusters — the cosmic web.

Why cross-correlate? The tSZ effect probes hot gas pressure integrated along the line of sight, while Euclid probes galaxy positions and shapes tracing the underlying matter distribution. Combining both breaks degeneracies and gives access to baryonic physics and cosmological parameters simultaneously.

The key idea: CMB photons that pass through galaxy clusters are inverse-Compton scattered by hot electrons, producing a characteristic spectral distortion — the thermal Sunyaev-Zeldovich (tSZ) effect. A Compton-y map traces the projected electron pressure, which correlates with galaxy overdensity and weak lensing convergence maps from Euclid.

Key Project: DR1-KP-CMBX-4

"Hot gas in the cosmic web via tSZ cross-correlation with galaxy clustering, weak lensing, and clusters"

This is one of the Euclid Collaboration's Key Projects under the CMBx Science Working Group (SWG). It targets constraints on cosmological parameters (especially $\Omega_m$, $\sigma_8$, $S_8$) and baryonic physics (pressure profile parameters) from the joint signal of the CMB and Euclid.

DR1 = Euclid Data Release 1 KP = Key Project CMBX = CMB × Euclid cross-correlation KP number 4

Cross-correlations targeted: $C_\ell^{yy}$ (tSZ auto), $C_\ell^{yg}$ (tSZ × galaxy clustering), $C_\ell^{y\ell}$ (tSZ × weak lensing)

Signal Taxonomy — What × What

There are two layers of naming here that often get conflated. Understanding their relationship clears up most of the confusion.

Key point: tSZ cross-correlations are a subset of CMBx. They are not two separate, parallel programs. "CMBx" is the working group label; "tSZ cross-correlations" describes which specific CMB map is used.

Layer 2 — The Euclid side: GC, WL, shear, convergence

The Euclid side also has terminology that overlaps confusingly:

Galaxy Clustering (GC)
δg(n̂, z)

The statistical distribution of galaxy positions across the sky, binned in redshift. Traces the underlying matter overdensity $\delta_m$ through galaxy bias $b_g$. This is a 2D map — not individual objects.

Weak Lensing (WL)
γ, κ

The phenomenon: gravity from foreground matter bends light paths, distorting the apparent shapes and sizes of background galaxies. WL is not a map itself — it is the physical effect that produces shear and convergence maps.

Shear
γ = γE + iγB

What you directly measure from WL: the distortion of galaxy ellipticities. Split into E-mode (physical lensing signal; curl-free) and B-mode (null test; must vanish for pure lensing). WL and shear are two names for essentially the same observable.

Convergence
κ(n̂)

A derived quantity related to shear by a mathematical transformation in Fourier space. $\kappa \propto \int \delta_m\, W^\kappa\, dl$ — it directly traces projected matter density. Shear and convergence carry identical information, just in different bases.

Galaxy Clusters
M > 1014 M☉

Individual massive objects detected in a catalog — not the same as galaxy clustering. Clusters are important because they dominate the tSZ signal (hottest, densest objects), but KP4 cross-correlates the tSZ y-map with the continuous $\delta_g$ field, not a cluster catalog.

WL ≈ Shear ≈ Convergence
all related

In practice: "tSZ × WL", "tSZ × shear", "tSZ × convergence" all refer to the same cross-correlation $C_\ell^{y\kappa}$. The three terms describe the same signal from different angles.

CMB Probes vs Euclid Probes

The two instrument families probe different aspects of the same underlying cosmic structure. Understanding which is which is essential to interpreting the cross-correlation signal.

CMB Probes — Hot Gas Pressure

tSZ / Compton-y Maps

Produced from CMB temperature maps by extracting the tSZ spectral distortion. They directly trace the integrated electron pressure along the line of sight, which is dominated by hot gas in massive halos (galaxy clusters and groups).

Sources:

  • Planck PR4 — full-sky Compton-y map (current analysis uses this)
  • ACT — Atacama Cosmology Telescope, higher resolution
  • SPT / SO — South Pole Telescope / Simons Observatory

Variants: with CIB deprojection (removes Cosmic Infrared Background contamination) or without; Jackknife maps for null tests.

Observable: $y(\hat{n})$ — Compton-y parameter map

Euclid Probes — Galaxy Distribution & Shapes

Galaxy Clustering & Weak Lensing

Derived from the Euclid galaxy catalog using photometric and spectroscopic data. They trace the matter density field — both through galaxy positions (clustering) and galaxy shape distortions (lensing).

Probes:

  • GC map — Galaxy Clustering; galaxy overdensity $\delta_g(\hat{n}, z)$ in tomographic bins
  • WL E-mode — Weak Lensing E-mode shear (physical signal; curl-free)
  • WL B-mode — Weak Lensing B-mode shear (null test; divergence-free)

Produced via the cat2map pipeline from the Euclid galaxy catalog. Current data release: RR2 (Quick Release 2). Upcoming: DR1.

Observables: $\delta_g(\hat{n})$, $\gamma_E(\hat{n})$, $\gamma_B(\hat{n})$

The tSZ Effect & Compton-y Parameter

Physical Picture

When CMB photons travel through a galaxy cluster, they encounter a hot plasma of electrons ($T_e \sim 10^7$–$10^8\,\mathrm{K}$). These electrons inverse-Compton scatter the CMB photons, boosting their energy. This shifts the CMB spectrum: photon intensity decreases at low frequencies ($\nu < 217\,\mathrm{GHz}$) and increases at high frequencies ($\nu > 217\,\mathrm{GHz}$). At 217 GHz the effect is exactly zero — the tSZ null frequency.

Key property: The tSZ signal is redshift-independent in amplitude (for a fixed mass). This makes it a powerful probe of the total thermal energy in the Universe across cosmic time.

The Compton-y Parameter

The Compton-y parameter quantifies the tSZ signal strength along a line of sight $\hat{n}$. It is the integral of the electron pressure $P_e = n_e k_B T_e$ along the physical path length $dl$:

(Compton-y) $$y(\hat{n}) = \frac{\sigma_T}{m_e c^2} \int P_e(\hat{n}, l)\, dl = \frac{\sigma_T}{m_e c^2} \int n_e(\hat{n}, l)\, k_B T_e(\hat{n}, l)\, dl$$

where:

  • $\sigma_T = 6.652 \times 10^{-29}\,\mathrm{m}^2$ — Thomson scattering cross-section
  • $m_e c^2 = 511\,\mathrm{keV}$ — electron rest-mass energy
  • $n_e$ — free electron number density
  • $T_e$ — electron temperature
  • $P_e = n_e k_B T_e$ — electron pressure

CMB Temperature Distortion

The tSZ effect modifies the CMB intensity and temperature as a function of frequency $\nu$:

(spectral distortion) $$\frac{\Delta I_\nu}{I_0} = g(\nu)\, y, \qquad \frac{\Delta T_\mathrm{CMB}}{T_\mathrm{CMB}} = f(\nu)\, y$$

where the spectral functions are:

(spectral functions) $$f(\nu) = x\coth\!\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) - 4, \qquad x \equiv \frac{h\nu}{k_B T_\mathrm{CMB}}$$

At $\nu \approx 217\,\mathrm{GHz}$: $f(\nu) = 0$ — the null frequency. At $\nu \lesssim 100\,\mathrm{GHz}$: $f(\nu) \approx -2$ — decrement (cluster appears cold). At $\nu \gtrsim 350\,\mathrm{GHz}$: $f(\nu) > 0$ — increment (cluster appears hot).

How tSZ and Euclid Probes Connect

The tSZ effect and Euclid tracers share the same underlying structure: dark matter halos. Hot gas (tSZ) and galaxies (GC/WL) are both hosted by halos of the same mass, at the same locations in the cosmic web. This creates a physical correlation that is quantified by the angular cross-power spectrum $C_\ell^{yX}$ where $X \in \{g, \ell\}$.

Halo Model Framework

All theoretical predictions are computed within the halo model: every galaxy, galaxy group, and cluster lives inside a dark matter halo. The signal from any tracer field can be decomposed into contributions from within a single halo (1-halo term) and between pairs of halos (2-halo term).

Matter Power Spectrum

The halo model decomposes the matter power spectrum as: $P(k) = P^{1h}(k) + P^{2h}(k)$ where the 1-halo term captures the correlation between particles in the same halo (small scales), and the 2-halo term captures correlations between different halos (large scales, tracing linear theory).

General Angular Power Spectrum

For any two tracers $A$ and $B$, the angular power spectrum using the Limber approximation is:

(Limber) $$C_\ell^{AB} = \int dz\, \frac{dV}{dz \, d\Omega} \int dM\, \frac{dn}{dM}(M,z)\, \tilde{u}^A_\ell(M,z)\, \tilde{u}^B_\ell(M,z) \quad \text{[1-halo]}$$
(2-halo) $$C_\ell^{AB, 2h} = \int dz\, \frac{dV}{dz \, d\Omega} \, b^A_\ell(z)\, b^B_\ell(z)\, P_\mathrm{lin}(k_\ell, z)$$

where the effective large-scale bias weighted profile is:

$$b^A_\ell(z) = \int dM\, \frac{dn}{dM}(M,z)\, b(M,z)\, \tilde{u}^A_\ell(M,z)$$

and:

  • $dn/dM$ — halo mass function (HMF); Tinker et al. (2008), $M_{200c}$
  • $b(M,z)$ — halo bias (Tinker et al. 2010)
  • $P_\mathrm{lin}(k,z)$ — linear matter power spectrum (Planck 2018 cosmology)
  • $\tilde{u}^A_\ell(M,z)$ — Fourier-space profile of tracer $A$ (see below)

Tracer Profiles $\tilde{u}^A_\ell$

tSZ (y tracer) — hot gas electron pressure

(y-profile) $$\tilde{u}^y_\ell(M,z) = \frac{4\pi r_s}{\ell_s^2}\, \frac{\sigma_T}{m_e c^2} \int_0^\infty dx\, x^2\, P_e(x, M, z)\, \frac{\sin(\ell x / \ell_s)}{\ell x / \ell_s}$$

where $x = r/r_s$ is the dimensionless radius, $\ell_s = D_A(z)/r_s$, and $P_e(x, M, z)$ follows the generalised NFW (GNFW) pressure profile of Arnaud et al. (2010) [A10]:

(GNFW) $$P_e(x) = \frac{P_0}{(c_{500} x)^\gamma \left[1 + (c_{500} x)^\alpha\right]^{(\beta-\gamma)/\alpha}}$$

with parameters $[P_0, c_{500}, \gamma, \alpha, \beta] = [8.403, 1.177, 0.3081, 1.0510, 5.4905]$ from A10 (universal pressure profile from X-ray observations of 33 clusters).

Galaxy Clustering (g tracer) — HOD

Galaxies are distributed within halos via the Halo Occupation Distribution (HOD). The profile in Fourier space is:

(HOD profile) $$\tilde{u}^g_\ell(M,z) = \frac{N_\mathrm{gal}(M)}{\bar{n}_g(z)}\, \tilde{u}^\mathrm{NFW}_\ell(M,z)$$

where $N_\mathrm{gal}(M) = N_c(M) + N_s(M)$ is the mean galaxy occupation:

  • $N_c(M) = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 + \mathrm{erf}\!\left(\frac{\log M - \log M_\mathrm{min}}{\sigma_{\log M}}\right)\right]$ — central galaxies
  • $N_s(M) = N_c(M)\left(\frac{M - M_0}{M_1'}\right)^\alpha$ — satellite galaxies
  • HOD parameters from Kusiak et al. (2022): $M_\mathrm{min} = 6.25\times10^{11}\,M_\odot/h$, $M_1' = 5.03\times10^{12}\,M_\odot/h$

Weak Lensing (κ tracer)

The lensing convergence $\kappa$ probes the projected matter density weighted by a geometric lensing kernel $W^\kappa(z, z_s)$:

$$\tilde{u}^\ell_\ell(M,z) = W^\kappa(z)\, \tilde{u}^\mathrm{NFW}_\ell(M,z), \qquad W^\kappa(z) = \frac{3H_0^2 \Omega_m}{2c^2}\, \frac{(1+z)\, \chi(z)}{\bar{\chi}}\, \int_z^\infty dz_s\, n(z_s)\, \frac{\chi(z_s)-\chi(z)}{\chi(z_s)}$$

Angular Power Spectra

The three main angular power spectra computed in this analysis are:

1. tSZ Auto-Spectrum $C_\ell^{yy}$

$$C_\ell^{yy} = C_\ell^{yy,1h} + C_\ell^{yy,2h}$$

Dominated by massive clusters at $z \lesssim 0.5$. Sensitive to $\sigma_8$, $\Omega_m$, and the pressure profile shape.

2. tSZ × Galaxy Clustering $C_\ell^{yg}$

$$C_\ell^{yg} = C_\ell^{yg,1h} + C_\ell^{yg,2h}$$

Cross-correlates gas pressure with galaxy overdensity. Sensitive to the galaxy-pressure connection: HOD, baryonic feedback, galaxy bias $b_g$.

3. tSZ × Weak Lensing $C_\ell^{y\ell}$

$$C_\ell^{y\ell} = C_\ell^{y\ell,1h} + C_\ell^{y\ell,2h}$$

Cross-correlates gas pressure with matter convergence. Breaks degeneracies between $\sigma_8$ and mass bias $b_H$ (hydrostatic mass bias).

Modelling codes: Theory spectra are computed with CLASS-SZ (Bolliet et al. 2023, 2024) and/or GODMAX (Pandey et al. 2024) — a JAX-based halo model framework. Both implement the 1h + 2h decomposition with the same physical inputs. See GODMAX notes.

NaMaster — Decoupled Power Spectra

Real sky maps have masks (Galactic plane, point sources, survey footprint). Masking couples different multipoles $\ell$ together, producing a pseudo-$C_\ell$. NaMaster (Alonso et al. 2019) corrects for this using a mode-coupling matrix $\mathcal{K}_{\ell\ell'}$:

(NaMaster) $$\tilde{C}_\ell = \sum_{\ell'} \mathcal{K}_{\ell\ell'}\, C_{\ell'} + \tilde{N}_\ell + \tilde{b}_\ell$$

where:

  • $\tilde{C}_\ell$ — observed (pseudo-) power spectrum on the masked sky
  • $\mathcal{K}_{\ell\ell'}$ — mode-coupling kernel (depends only on the mask geometry)
  • $C_{\ell'}$ — true (decoupled) power spectrum we want to recover
  • $\tilde{N}_\ell$ — noise bias
  • $\tilde{b}_\ell$ — beam/pixel window bias

Inversion: We solve for $C_\ell$ by applying the inverse mode-coupling matrix, giving the decoupled (unbiased) power spectrum estimate.

Modelling

The theoretical prediction for the observed cross-spectrum must account for:

  1. Halo model: Compute $C_\ell^{yg}$ from 1h + 2h decomposition (GODMAX or CLASS-SZ)
  2. Projection effects: Integrate over the window functions for each tomographic bin $i$: $W^g_i(z)$ for galaxy clustering, $W^y(z)$ for tSZ
  3. NaMaster coupling: Apply the mode-coupling matrix $\mathcal{K}_{\ell\ell'}$ to the theoretical $C_\ell$ before comparing with data
  4. Noise bias: Add noise power spectrum $N_\ell^{yy}$, $N_\ell^{gg}$
  5. Simulation calibration: Flagship Euclid simulations calibrate the HOD and multiplicative shear bias

Sampling — NUTS × GODMAX

Bayesian parameter estimation is performed using the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS), a variant of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo that automatically adapts step sizes. The theory code is GODMAX — written in JAX, enabling:

  • Automatic differentiation → exact gradients for NUTS (no finite-difference approximations)
  • JIT compilation → fast likelihood evaluation
  • GPU acceleration → many parallel chains

Likelihood

The likelihood is Gaussian in the data vector $\mathbf{d} = \{\tilde{C}_\ell^{yg,i}\}_{i=1}^6$:

$$\ln \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\frac{1}{2} \left(\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{t}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\right)^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \left(\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{t}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\right)$$

where $\mathbf{t}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the theory prediction (GODMAX → NaMaster coupling) and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is the full covariance matrix (analytical + simulation-validated).

Preliminary Results

First detection: tSZ × Galaxy Clustering ($C_\ell^{yg}$) measured with Planck PR4 × Euclid RR2, showing overall agreement with the halo model prediction. This is an early-stage result using both Q1 and RR2_v2.0 galaxy samples.

Key features of the measurement:

  • $C_\ell^{yg}$ measured for all 6 tomographic bins across angular multipoles $\ell \sim 100$–$2000$
  • Theory (GODMAX halo model) shows good agreement with the data vector in amplitude and shape
  • Residuals being studied: deviations may indicate baryonic feedback, photo-z uncertainties, or systematics
  • Covariance matrix includes both Gaussian (analytic) and non-Gaussian (simulation) contributions
Caution: These are preliminary results. Systematic tests (null tests, robustness checks) are still ongoing. Unblinding and publication require all systematic checks to pass.
